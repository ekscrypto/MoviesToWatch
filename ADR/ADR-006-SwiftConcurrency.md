Decision: The app has fully embraced Swift Concurrency. New asynchronous work uses `Task` and `AsyncStream` (or `AsyncSequence` variants from `swift-async-algorithms`); Grand Central Dispatch (`DispatchQueue`, `DispatchQueue.async`, `DispatchQueue.asyncAfter`, `dispatch_*`) and Combine (`PassthroughSubject`, `CurrentValueSubject`, `.sink`, `.throttle`, `.debounce`) must not be introduced in domain or activity code. The UI-layer `ViewRep` observation is also Combine-free: `StateMachine.viewReps` is an `AsyncStream<ViewRep>` consumed by SwiftUI (`.task { for await … }`) and by the test scaffolding.

Reasoning: A single concurrency model across the codebase gives the compiler full visibility for sendability and actor-isolation checking, removes the GCD ↔ Task hop on every emission, and makes cancellation structural — cancelling an `Activity`'s `Task` tears down everything downstream. Streamed observers (search ticks, persistence flushes, state-machine fan-out) are expressed as `AsyncStream` consumed by a single structured `Task` whose lifetime is tied to the owning `Activity`; throttling, debouncing, and coalescing come from `AsyncThrottleSequence` / `AsyncDebounceSequence` rather than Combine operators dispatched on `DispatchQueue.global()`. Delays use `try await Task.sleep(...)` instead of `DispatchQueue.asyncAfter`. This keeps the codebase consistent with ADR-001 (StateMachine as the single serial mutation point) and ADR-008 (tests observe `ViewRep` via the async stream, not sleep loops).

## Review Scope

**Strengthens** findings about newly introduced GCD primitives (`DispatchQueue.async`, `asyncAfter`, `dispatch_*`), Combine types (`PassthroughSubject`, `CurrentValueSubject`, `.sink`, `.throttle`, `.debounce`), or hybrid GCD ↔ Task hops in domain, activity, or UI-observation code. Cite this ADR when a change adds a non–Swift Concurrency code path or breaks structural cancellation by detaching work from its owning `Task`. **Drops** complaints that the Swift Concurrency idiom is "non-standard" or that GCD/Combine would be terser — the codebase has formally chosen Swift Concurrency and uniformity across modules is the goal.
